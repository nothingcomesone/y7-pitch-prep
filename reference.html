The 6 elements of your pitch

Repeat elements ① – ⑥ TWICE — once for each family member or pet.

Optional opener (not graded, but encouraged):
Nǐ hǎo! Wǒ jiào ___.  — Hello! My name is ___.
①Identify
Zhè shì wǒ de ___.

②Name
Wǒ de ___ jiào ___.
My ___ is called ___. (English names are OK.)
③Age ⚠ no shì!
Wǒ de ___ ___ suì.
My ___ is ___ years old. (Never: Wǒ de bàba shì 45 suì)
④Nationality ⚠ rén required!
Tā shì ___ rén.
He / She is ___ (Chinese / Australian / American / British / Japanese). Never: Tā shì Àodàlìyà.
⑤Description
Wǒ de ___ hěn ___, [hé / dànshì] hěn ___.
1 appearance + 1 personality. Link with hé (and) or dànshì (but). Always say hěn before the adjective!
⑥Location
Tā zhù zài ___.
He / She lives in ___ (Xīní / Běijīng / Lúndūn / Shànghǎi).
Optional close (not graded):
Xièxie. Zàijiàn!  — Thank you. Goodbye!

The 5 deadly errors — memorise!

  1. Forgetting hěn before an adjective — Chinese needs hěn as glue. Tā hěn gāo, never Tā gāo.
  2. No shì with age — say ___ suì, never shì ___ suì.
  3. Always end nationality with rén (人). Tā shì Àodàlìyà rén.
  4. Word order: hěn FIRST, then adjective. Never gāo hěn.
  5. Location needs zhù zài — never just zhù.

Grammar in detail

Why hěn? — the rule behind element ⑤

In English we say "My dad is tall." The verb "is" connects the person to the adjective. Chinese does NOT do this. Chinese adjectives are already verb-like — they mean "is/are tall," "is/are smart" all by themselves. So we never say shì before an adjective.

But there's a catch: if you just say "Wǒ de bàba gāo", a Chinese listener hears half a sentence — like "My dad is taller…" and waits for the comparison ("…than my brother"). To stop that, we put hěn in front. Hěn here is a marker — it does NOT really mean "very." Treat it as the glue that turns "tall" into a finished sentence.

So the pattern is fixed: Subject + hěn + adjective. Always in that order. Never "gāo hěn". Never "shì hěn gāo". Never "hěn de gāo".

shì vs hěn — when to use which

shì (是) — for NOUNS only hěn (很) — for ADJECTIVES
✓ Tā shì lǎoshī. (He is a teacher.) ✓ Tā hěn cōngming. (He is smart.)
✓ Tā shì Àodàlìyà rén. (She is Australian.) ✓ Tā hěn gāo. (He is tall.)
✓ Wǒ shì xuéshēng. (I am a student.) ✓ Wǒ hěn kuàilè. (I am happy.)

hé (and) — same flavour

Use hé to join two qualities of the same flavour — both positive, both physical, both about personality. Think "addition."

  • ✓ Wǒ de bàba hěn gāo hé hěn dà. (Tall AND big — both physical.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de gǒu hěn xiǎo hé hěn kě'ài. (Small AND cute — both adorable.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de mèimei hěn cōngming hé hěn kuàilè. (Smart AND happy — both positive.)

dànshì (but) — different flavour

Use dànshì to join two contrasting qualities — one positive and one negative, or two clearly different types. Think "however."

  • ✓ Wǒ de gēge hěn dà dànshì hěn kě'ài. (Big BUT cute — tough vs. soft.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de māma hěn cōngming dànshì hěn yánlì. (Smart BUT strict — likeable vs. tough.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de dìdi hěn xiǎo dànshì hěn cōngming. (Small BUT smart — physical vs. mental.)

⚠ Non-negotiable: repeat hěn before the second adjective!

✓ hěn gāo hé hěn cōngming

✗ hěn gāo hé cōngming   ✗ hěn gāo dànshì cōngming

👉 Decision rule (1 line)

Same flavour? → hé.   Different flavour? → dànshì.
